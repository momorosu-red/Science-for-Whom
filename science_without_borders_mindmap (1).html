Science W/out Borders

Science is not neutral.

Science advanced. Access to the basics didn't.

Examining the systems that science and scientists operate within is how we learn to widen access to science and sharpen its ability to meet the world's most urgent needs. This page lays out that case in six parts, drawing on the research and history that shaped both progress and disparities.

Mission

"Everyone has the right ... to share in scientific advancement and its benefits." Article 27, UN Declaration of Human Rights (1948)

I

Science and scientists operate within systems of power that maintain the oppressive ideologies they were founded on.

II

Internal and external forces threaten scientific discovery and its impact.

III

Active global crises require the attention and action of the scientific community.

IV

There are skills and paths available to future scientists that can help them make a difference.

A Few Questions First

Before the map — a quick, honest orientation to what this actually is.

A mindmap and growing collection of research examining how power, history, and access shape modern science — built by three McGill science students who wanted a clearer answer to one question: why doesn't scientific progress reach everyone equally?

No — it's the opposite. Believing in science means being honest about the systems it operates inside. Naming those systems doesn't undermine science; ignoring them does.

Anyone curious about how science actually works — students, faculty, or just someone who ended up here. No background in global health or science studies required.
